\section{Introduction}\label{intro}

Many important ideas in science are conveyed through formulas. For instance, in computer science, formulas define the statistical models we use to test our hypotheses, provide the backbone of many proofs of correctness, and formally describe new techniques in systems and AI models. However, formulas are not always easy to understand. Professionals have described notation as a barrier to learning and reading~\cite{Cai2019-lb,ref:mysore2023how}, and teachers have described issues in its use as students' progress to engineering maturity~\cite{faulkner2019mathematical}. When formulas are not appropriately introduced, they have the potential to reduce rather than support comprehension~\cite{Osterholm2006-al,leung1997learning}.

Perhaps to address some of these issues, math communicators have experimented with various ways to present formulas so that their meaning may be more self-evident. These go beyond the kinds of graphs one might see on the chalkboard of an algebra class or on a student's graphing calculator. In interactive textbooks \cite{margalit2019interactive, poncecampuzano2019complex} and publications\cite{distill2017}, formulas have become part of narrative texts where they are inter-linked with sentences, simulations, and visualizations. The formula can be a dynamic object where readers can plug in values and where visual references are established between parts of the formula and visuals representing mathematical phenomena.

From an implementation perspective, tying formulas into interactive math narratives can be an involved process. For instance, to do this in a web document, an author would need to write math markup and write code to recover components for variables from rendered formulas, listen to interaction events, reactively update the variables, and handle layout aspects. Markup tools for augmenting static math formulas have been described by some as clunky and prone to poor outputs~\cite{Head2022-ws}; 
%these observations were for static augmentations so one might imagine the situation of 
dynamic augmentations would be even more complex. Indeed, some authors have indicated desire for better tools for dynamic formula presentations~\cite{Head2022-ws}.

To help authors find the right techniques for describing their formulas, we design a new domain-specific language (DSL). This DSL makes formulas richly interactive in scientific documents authored for the web. We call the DSL \emph{Delta}, reflecting the dynamism we seek to bring to symbols. Going beyond prior toolkits for augmenting notation~\cite{Wu2023-xc,ref:li2022heartdown,manim3b1b}, ours offers low-threshold methods for adding calculation, linked visuals, and walkthrough explanations to formulas. It offers a high ceiling, for instance permitting incorporation of graphics in place of expressions and supporting linking of systems of formulas and visuals (Figure~\ref{fig:teaser}). The DSL's design aims to support crispness of specification with a clear separation of concerns between the markup for a formula, specification of variable interactivity, and specification of formula semantics. Meanwhile, it offers constructs for providing step-based explanations and connecting to complementary text and visuals.

This paper makes two contributions. The first is the design of Delta, comprising a set of constructs aimed for authoring of dynamic formulas in interactive documents. The second contribution is evidence of fit to envisioned task. Twenty users across two studies augmented formulas using Delta. In the first study, they were able to make changes to a formula spec more quickly and easily than with a React baseline that was  streamlined to support the formula augmentation task. The second study demonstrated readers' ability to leverage the constructs in a walk-up-and-use situation where they added calculation and walkthroughs to formulas according to their personal designs. Altogether, this paper takes an important step towards developing an important kind of language for future scientific authoring environments.
