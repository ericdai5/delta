\subsection{Method}

We surveyed interactive math content across established sources including Distill.pub, Setosa.io, Explorable Explanations, complex-analysis.com, Georgia Tech's Interactive Linear Algebra textbook, and individual developer sites. We first identified exhibits that present a formula in a multiple-representation or interactive context, then one author developed a coding scheme iteratively through open coding of the first ten examples before applying the stabilized scheme to the remainder. For each of 46 instances across 35 unique pages, we coded six dimensions \jeff{I think it's more appropriate to call these ``paterns'' than ``dimensions''}.

\subsection{Findings}

Three findings directly informed the design of Delta.

\subsubsection{Finding 1: Dynamic formulas in interactive math 
exhibits are common.}

More than half of instances (25 of 46) featured formula values that 
update in response to interaction. This suggests that authors already 
view formulas as living objects rather than static \LaTeX{} 
renderings, and that readers benefit from seeing how changing one 
quantity ripples through an expression. Yet as later findings show, 
the mechanisms for driving these updates remain indirect and 
inconsistent.

\subsubsection{Finding 2: Most formulas are linked to 
visualizations.}

29 of 46 instances (63\%) established some form of linked selection 
between a formula and an accompanying visualization. Formulas and 
graphs frequently co-occur in mathematical exposition, and authors 
are clearly investing effort to connect them. However, as noted in 
our earlier analysis of directionality, the vast majority of these 
links run in only one direction, limiting readers' ability to 
translate freely between representations.

\subsubsection{Finding 3: Some exhibits use step-by-step 
revelation.}

3 instances used step controls to walk readers through a 
visualization incrementally, almost all in the context of graphs and 
vectors. Although uncommon, this pattern points to an authorial 
desire to scaffold complex ideas by revealing structure gradually 
rather than presenting a fully formed formula all at once.
