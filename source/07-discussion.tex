\section{Discussion}

Our studies provide evidence for the usability of Delta in walk-up-and-use settings. The first study showed greater speed and ease with Delta than a React baseline. The second study showed participants producing formula calculators and walkthroughs from scratch in 30-minute periods, with constructs largely being reported as somewhat or very intuitive. Participants regularly picked up and used features for referencing variables, defining semantics and steps, and configuring interactivity. These findings suggest the fit of the basic design of Delta for basic tasks.

\subsection{Study limitations}

Our study involved participants who are not experienced math document authors, and there may be additional gaps to uncover for this group. Tasks were relatively short (maxing out at 30 minutes) and the possibilities of exploration were limited by the equations provided. Further studies of unconstrained use are necessary to determine if the language can support the formulas and goals that authors bring to the tables themselves.

\subsection{Future Work}

The Delta language, and ideas from its development, open up several avenues for interesting future research.

\paragraph{Interactions around structured expressions.}
How should a DSL expose mechanisms for readers to interactively modify complex expressions like vectors, matrices, or indexed sets? Currently, Delta requires authors to expand these structures in the formula and mark their members as individual variables to permit any sort of interaction. An alternative could be to represent these structures by single variables (e.g., ``$\vec{v}$,'' ``$A$''), and allow readers to unpack and interact with them in the margins. An interesting direction to explore is defining control widgets corresponding to these various data types. In some places, these control widgets may be compositional, as in a vector of strings that would allow one to change the list of elements and expose widgets for modifying individual member strings.

\paragraph{Symbolic expansion and derivation.} Delta is optimized for calculations involving \emph{concrete} values. However, manipulation of formulas happens in contexts without concrete values. For instance, formulas are manipulated to derive new formulas. They are also expanded into finer terms, like when one lists out the terms of a Taylor series. It could be useful to allow authors to support interactive expansion and derivation. We conjecture that this might require integration of Delta-like DSLs with engines for symbolic manipulation (e.g., sympy), and constructs for pointing out what to expand and provide the potential for recursive expansion of terms.

\medskip

Additionally, we envision possibilities in Delta accelerating human-AI authoring of interactive math documents and in scaffolding the creation of graphical editing tools that produce interactive formulas.
