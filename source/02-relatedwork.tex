\section{Background and Related Work}

% In this section, we discuss why authors might wish to augment and enliven mathematical notation paired with dynamic representations, and then situate our system amid related work and tools. 

\subsection{Tools to understand notation}

Reading notation is at times involved and fraught. Learners have described it as an impediment to self-driven learning in domains like machine learning~\cite{Cai2019-lb}. Notation has been correlated with lower assessments in comprehension tasks~\cite{Osterholm2006-al}. Understanding notation often depends on complementary representations in the text; \citet{Kohlhase2018-vv} observed readers frequently shifting focus between formulas and nearby prose. Presentation changes affect reading: visual cues have been associated with lower perceived cognitive load in mathematics tasks~\cite{5c7148d6-681c-3534-885c-7936f89f5133}, and spacing can influence how expressions are interpreted~\cite{Landy2007-zk}. Researchers have experimented with interactive explanations and labels for notation~\cite{Head2020-eg,ref:alcock2011eproofs,crichton2021newmedium,ref:li2022heartdown,Chulpongsatorn_2023,Gunturu_2024}, finding they support definition acquisition in search tasks~\cite{Head2020-eg}. \citet{Head2022-ws} documented augmentations authors make to formulas in the wild, distilling common patterns of embedding of visualizations in formulas, styling and annotating formulas, and incorporating interaction. This paper is concerned with the latter category.

While we are not aware of prior studies of the effects of interactive calculation, visuals, and walkthroughs on formulas specifically, educational frameworks justify why expert implementations could benefit learners. \citet{Ainsworth2006-pu}'s DeFT framework explains how multiple representations support learning by providing complementary information, constraining interpretation, and helping learners construct deeper understanding. Walkthroughs can be viewed as segmentation: breaking complex material into learner-controllable chunks can improve comprehension under high cognitive load~\cite{Plass2009-sq}, consistent with multimedia learning accounts of limited-capacity processing~\cite{mayer2001multimedia}.

Of prior work developing interactive formulas, most focused on providing definitions on demand~\cite{Head2020-eg,crichton2021newmedium,ref:li2022heartdown}. One sought to relate formulas across a proof~\cite{ref:alcock2011eproofs}. Two projects~\cite{Chulpongsatorn_2023,Gunturu_2024} sought to make notation interactive in our sense---enabling calculation and visualization in place. These tools provided a small number of prescribed, automatically-generated augmentations; our intent is to capture those patterns among a richer set within a usable, configurable, and expressive authoring dialect.

\subsection{Tools for augmenting notation}

The most standard tool for augmenting notation is arguably LaTeX, which provides simple facilities for coloring \cite{color_package} and labeling formulas\cite{mathtools_package} as well as user-contributed packages\cite{annotate_equations}. In a study of authors who augmented formulas, \citet{Head2022-ws} reported authors finding LaTeX clunky and difficult for this task. Numerous experimental languages have been developed to make this easier. Nota~\cite{crichton2021newmedium}, Heartdown~\cite{ref:li2022heartdown}, and FFL~\cite{Wu2023-xc} provide DSLs for defining definitions apart from formula LaTeX and automatically associating them at render time. Individual tools go further: Heartdown supports automatic rendering of formulas in graph visualizations, and FFL provides color and styling rules that cut across a document. These tools share a separation between formula specification and presentation seen across programming (e.g., HTML/CSS) and recent DSLs like Penrose for mathematical diagram generation~\cite{ref:ye2020penrose}---a philosophy we carry into Delta.

A small number of community DSLs bring dynamism to formulas. \emph{manim}~\cite{manim3b1b} generates math animations where formulas are built up step-by-step but compiles to video without integrating into typeset documents. \emph{FigureOne}~\cite{figureone2018} supports animation and interaction with equations, but its primitives target visual aspects of mathematical diagrams rather than the semantic structure of formulas; it lacks built-in support for LaTeX-based formula description and concise constructs for attaching semantics, labels, and walkthroughs. With Delta, we aim to greatly expand the expressiveness of languages for presenting formulas with dedicated, flexible constructs for associating calculation to variables, formulas, and related document content.

Formulas can also be augmented through graphical editors; Freeform \cite{tao2025freeform} and i-LaTeX~\cite{ref:gobert2022ilatex} provide recent research instantiations of editing and annotating LaTeX through direct selection, and such features appear in numerous products (e.g.,~\cite{microsoft_equation_editor,Topping1999UsingMT,tool:mathcha}). We envision our DSL as a material that, when integrated into such tools, could enable direct authoring of interactive formulas.

\subsection{Tools to create explorables and explanations}

We see Delta as one tool fitting into ecosystems for authoring effective technical explanations. It can be considered a material for building explorable explanations, which Victor~\cite{victor2011explorable} describes as documents that help readers learn by tinkering with assumptions and observing consequences. The calculation interactions central to Delta are a common piece of DSLs for explorable explanations broadly---a store of variable values, with anchors in the text serving as controls for editing those variables and propagating changes elsewhere in the document. This pattern appears in Potluck~\cite{potluck2022}, Idyll~\cite{ref:conlen2018idyll}, and Tangle.js~\cite{tangle2011}. Our intent is to make these interactions work crisply for formulas with a wide range of expressive possibilities, requiring an implementation that plugs interactivity and value rendering directly into the formula and an exploration of a broad space of walkthrough and demonstration methods. In its intent, Delta is analogous to recent math and science diagramming dialects like Penrose~\cite{ref:ye2020penrose} and Bloom~\cite{Teller2025-pw}, Bluefish~\cite{ref:pollock2022bluefish} and Chisel~\cite{chisel}, making good designs of technical artifacts naturally follow from a language fit to task.
