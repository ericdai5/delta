\section{Observation Study}\label{observational}

We conducted an observation study in which authors used Delta to design and build interactive formulas from scratch for two different mathematical concepts. This study targeted two additional research questions:

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{RQ3.} How do authors use Delta to build interactive formulas in a long-form, open-ended authoring task?\\
\noindent\textbf{RQ4.} Which features of the library do authors find intuitive and where do they encounter difficulty?
\vspace{0.5em}

\subsection{Methodology}

\paragraph{authors.}
Ten of the 19 authors from the controlled study (Section \ref{results}) took part in an observation study. authors rated their comfort writing \LaTeX{} formulas as a median of 4 on a 5-point Likert scale ($\sigma = 1.03$); 6 wrote \LaTeX{} weekly, 1 monthly, and 3 less than monthly. They rated their comfort writing JavaScript as a median of 3.5 ($\sigma = 1.43$); 5 wrote JS weekly, 3 monthly, and 1 daily. Regarding React experience, 5 had professional experience, 6 had personal or academic experience, and 3 had limited personal or academic experience. We refer to these authors by the same anonymized identifiers used in the controlled study (e.g., P21, P48).

\paragraph{Tasks.}
Each author completed two open-ended formula authoring tasks, each lasting up to 30 minutes. Their choice of formula and the provided training is meant to better assess specific parts of the Delta config structure. Task 1 (T1) assessed the basic interaction and notation features, while Task 2 (T2) evaluated the stepping syntax. \andrew{why viz and SVG features not included?}

\paragraph{Training.}
Prior to the tasks, each author completed two written tutorials that introduced the library's features incrementally. T1 walked authors through how to build an interactive formula from scratch using a gravitational force example ($\vec{F} = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$). It covered defining a formula, labeling variables, formatting displayed values, defining semantics, and making variables interactive. T2 built on the same formula and introduced walkthroughs: enabling stepping mode, adding \texttt{step()} calls, highlighting expressions, and formatting intermediate values with the \texttt{latex()} helper. authors worked through these tutorials at their own pace within the 20 minute limit.

\paragraph{Setup.} Study sessions took place remotely over Zoom and were recorded. authors used their own computers to retain familiar input configurations, and logged into GitHub in Google Chrome using their personal accounts. Each task had a dedicated branch in a GitHub Codespace — an online IDE — pre-loaded with template code. Tutorials and task instructions were shared as published Notion pages. Survey data was collected via Google Forms within the same browser session.

\paragraph{Duration.}
Each task lasted up to 30 minutes, after which the facilitator stopped the author. \andrew{Does this mean many authors got cut off before finishing something serious?} Including tutorials, questionnaires, and transitions, each session lasted approximately 2 hours.

\paragraph{Measurement and Analysis.}
9 of the 10 sessions were facilitated and recorded by the same researcher; 1 session was facilitated by a second researcher following the same protocol. During the task, authors were told to think-aloud ~\cite{ref:ericsson1984protocol} \andrew{broken cite} and focus on authoring and that the facilitator might interrupt them with questions about their process from time-to-time. Otherwise, the facilitator did not answer questions about the library unless a author was stuck for an extended period.

We also asked them to reflect on their experience in post-task questionnaires after each task. The questionaire asked authors about the usefulness of each of Delta's main features relevant to that specific task. The questionnaire assesses the intuitiveness and difficulty of features on a 5-point Likert scale. authors also provided free-text explanations for notable ratings.

After the session, both researchers independently reviewed their respective recordings and compiled timestamped observation logs for each author, documenting feature usage patterns, error sequences, recovery strategies, and notable think-aloud utterances. One researcher then performed a qualitative coding procedure, a pass over the full dataset, identifying grouped them into common observed workflows, identifying design successes (P--, P--), and design gaps (P--,P--) in a process inspired by thematic analysis \andrew{weirdly vague}. The codes that were retained were those that were seen as helpful insights for future DSL design.

\subsubsection{Observed Workflows}

Delta supported two distinct authoring workflows across the 20 task sessions.

\paragraph{Incremental authoring through progressive evaluation.} 
At least 8 of 10 authors (P48, P11, P45, P57, P21, P29, P14, P19) regularly alternated between editing code and checking rendered output---a tight loop enabled by Delta's reactive propagation, which re-renders on every change without requiring a complete specification. Many authors (P48, P17, P14, P11) checked formula rendering before declaring any variables; as P17 noted, ``First thing I want to do is to get the formula to show up. Seeing something will help.'' This validates Delta's compositional design (Section~\ref{System}): authors began with a bare \LaTeX{} string, then progressively layered on variables, semantics, and visualizations. 6 of 10 authors applied a uniform scaffolding strategy---3 (P48, P29, P11) initialized empty variable objects and verified their existence by hovering on formula symbols, while 3 (P17, P45, P14) assigned descriptive names and checked that labels appeared at expected positions. Both strategies relied on variables being defined apart from formulas as universal indices (Section~\ref{Visualizations}), allowing authors to stub out variables minimally and immediately see their effect.

\paragraph{Pedagogical reasoning for iterating.}
Several authors (P48, P11, P19, P17, P29) paused to reason about pedagogical effectiveness. In T1, P48 concluded that ``some questions relate to how much time has passed,'' making time interactive. P19 chose not to make $\lambda$ draggable, reasoning ``lambda is a constant.'' P11 reasoned about physical constraints: ``Time should never be negative. Let's have a range for that.'' P29 said ``For the first step I can define a set of possible values X and on the second step I can decompose the sum.'' These observations indicate that Delta's primitives---variable settings (range, step, default) and the stepping API---gave authors a vocabulary for expressing pedagogical intent.

\subsubsection{Design Successes}\label{design-successes}

We organize design successes using the Cognitive Dimensions of Notations framework~\cite{Blackwell2003-gv}, which provides a vocabulary for evaluating the usability of notational systems like DSLs. We focus on two dimensions that emerged most clearly: \emph{viscosity} (effort to make small changes) and \emph{closeness of mapping} (how naturally the notation maps to the problem domain).

\paragraph{S1: Viscosity.}
Delta's config-driven structure---formulas, variables, semantics---provided a scaffold that authors found natural to iterate upon. In 16 of 20 sessions, authors followed the configuration's structure: LaTeX first, then variables, then semantics. P45 treated her own tutorial code as a template reference rather than the tutorial document. Two authors (P57, P19) deviated by writing semantics before fully specifying variables---P19 explained: ``Once I started writing everything in the semantics area, I would know which vars I need''---yet both completed their tasks, suggesting the scaffold accommodates different workflows. Post-task ratings reflect this (Figure~\ref{fig:obs-task1}): creating the static formula and labeling a variable were unanimously rated ``very intuitive,'' and making a variable draggable was rated ``very intuitive'' by 8 of 10.

Authors (P80, P48, P17, P45, P11) found that declarative abstractions helped localize edits, letting them modify one aspect without ripple effects across the specification. P48 recognized she could change her computation to derive the initial substance amount from the final by editing only the semantics function, without adjusting variable declarations. P45 reiterated that ``having all the variable definitions in one place makes setting labels and ranges very simple.'' Low viscosity extended to debugging: P14 and P21 immediately used \texttt{console.log} within the semantics function to diagnose NaN values.

Delta's formatting utilities---\texttt{sigFigs}, \texttt{precision}, and \texttt{latex()}---were adopted successfully on first use as authors (P17, P19, P48, P57, P21, P14, P45) refined their displays. P57 discovered the API through the tutorial: ``I want to make $N(t)$ scientific notation\ldots\ ohhh, sigfigs. It works. Nice!'' Participants (P21, P14, P45) chained \texttt{latex()} with \texttt{sigfigs()} (P21, P45) or \texttt{precision()} (P14) to format step labels on their first attempts. Across both tasks, formatting followed a two-phase pattern: authors first focused on getting computation correct---defining variables, writing semantics, and verifying values---before returning to adjust presentation with significant figures, precision, or LaTeX rendering.

\paragraph{S2: Closeness of mapping.}
We observe closeness of mapping across the DSL's constructs.

\emph{LaTeX-based augmentations.} Variable declarations mapped closely to mathematical thinking: authors began by identifying symbols in their formula and translating each into a variable entry with an id, name, and default value. Labeling was rated ``very intuitive'' by 9 of 10 participants, and making a variable draggable by 8 of 10. P29 reasoned ``since there are special symbols I can call the variable id's with quotation marks''---correctly intuiting that variable ids correspond to LaTeX symbols. P21 noted ``it was very easy to define the variables and use them in formula and render.'' At least half of participants (P48, P17, P45, P57, P29, P14) correctly referenced complex variables like ``$N(t)$'' and ``$\lambda$'' on their first try. P80 initially wrote ``lambda'' but quickly self-corrected to ``\textbackslash lambda.'' Expression highlighting selectors also proved natural, with 7 of 10 authors (P48, P17, P19, P45, P80, P57, P14) successfully labeling an expression on their first attempt.

\emph{Computation semantics and stepping.} The semantics function---plain JavaScript---was well received: accessing variable values was rated ``very intuitive'' by 8 of 10 authors, and expressing computation by 7 of 10. While JavaScript's intuitiveness is unsurprising, the notable finding is how naturally its control flow transferred to structuring computations: \texttt{for} loops for summations (P57: ``Oh, I have to use a for loop---they all need to do the same thing''), \texttt{if} statements for case logic (P45), and local variables for readability. Embedding \texttt{step()} calls within ordinary JavaScript let authors express the \emph{order} and \emph{nesting} of pedagogical steps using familiar sequential structures---rated ``very intuitive'' by 6 of 10, notably lower than LaTeX-based augmentations, suggesting that translating pedagogical intent into sequential code is less immediately transparent. P48 noted ``placing steps [is] pretty intuitive because [it] follows how your brain thinks about math expressions,'' and P57 observed ``once the template of the formula was setup, writing the steps was fast.''

\subsubsection{Design Gaps}\label{design-gaps}

Our study also revealed several gaps in Delta's current design that future iterations should address.

\paragraph{G1: Variable disambiguation for repeated symbols.}
When a variable symbol appears multiple times in a formula---as $t$ does in $N(t) = N_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t}$---authors struggled to control which occurrence receives a label or interaction. In T1, 5 authors (P14, P17, P19, P29, P48) expressed frustration or surprise that the $t$ inside $N(t)$ was labeled rather than the standalone $t$ in the exponent. P48 and P19 wished the label would appear at the rightmost $t$. P48 and P14 discovered a workaround: using a space character in the variable key (e.g., \texttt{" t"}) to target a specific occurrence. While effective at labeling the rightmost $t$, this workaround is shown to be fragile when P14 later encountered a NaN error because his semantics referenced \texttt{vars.t} rather than \texttt{vars[" t"]}, creating a mismatch between the display workaround and the computation. This gap points to a need for explicit occurrence selection in the variable declaration, such as an index or a selector that specifies which occurrence of a symbol in the LaTeX should be bound to a given variable. \andrew{either skip or greatly shorten. can point out that FFL already provides a solution to this, could be added to Delta.}

\paragraph{G2: Stepping requires multiple non-obvious enabling steps.}
Activating the step-through feature requires three actions: setting \texttt{stepping: true} in the configuration, adding \texttt{step()} calls in the semantics function, and rendering a \texttt{StepControl} component in the page. Omitting any one of these produces no visible error---the formula simply renders without steps. A few people where stuck due to this error (P80, P11, P21). P80 spent over 9 minutes wondering ``I'm confused why there's no steps showing'' before discovering he had forgotten \texttt{stepping: true}. P11 became confused when he added \texttt{stepping: true} and semantics but saw no labels, because he had not yet added any step calls or the \texttt{StepControl} component. This pattern suggests that the stepping feature's activation feature should be simplified to require less spread-out actions from the author. \andrew{great. should be related back to closeness of mapping, as perhaps problem to be solved.}
