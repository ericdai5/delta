\section{Controlled Study}\label{results}

To evaluate the fit of Delta to the task, we conducted two studies. In the first study (this section), we compared participants' experience altering a formula augmentation with Delta and, as a baseline, a custom implemented React component library representing the current best alternative. In the second study (Section~\ref{observational}), we evaluated the learnability of a substantial subset of its constructs in a walk-up-and-use setting where participants implemented formula calculators and walkthroughs from scratch.

In this first controlled study, we sought to assess whether Delta reduced the time and effort involved in altering an existing augmentation. The choice to have participants alter formulas was made to allow comparison to a baseline; any baselines we attempted required dozens or hundreds of lines of boilerplate to implement simple interactive formulas, so we reduced complexity of task until the participants could accomplish it with the baseline during a study session. This study therefore assesses someone's ability to rapidly understand how to edit a formula specification.

\subsection{Methods}

\paragraph{Participants} We lacked a mechanism to recruit a sizable number of authors of interactive math documents, so instead we recruited those with the necessary prerequisite skills: competency with LaTeX formulas, JavaScript, and basic React. We recruited 19 graduate and undergraduate students from a private university's engineering school across computer science, data science, computer graphics, and robotics, and screened participants for these competencies. Participants rated comfort with LaTeX formulas a median of 4 on a 5-point Likert scale; and 4 for JavaScript; all had prior experience with React, ranging from small personal or academic projects to substantial professional use.\footnote{Further information about participants' background is in the appendix.}

\paragraph{Setup} Sessions took place remotely over Zoom and were recorded. Participants used their own computers. Tasks were run using GitHub Codespaces - an online IDE - pre-loaded with template code.

\paragraph{Tasks} The study was within-subjects: each participant completed the same formula editing task twice, once with each library (baseline and Delta). We chose to have participants complete the same task with both tools to enable direct comparison of implementation efficiency for identical functionality. Tool order was counterbalanced (9 used baseline first, 10 used Delta first) to mitigate learning effects. The complexity of the task primarily stemmed from identifying and using the correct constructs in each library to achieve the desired functionality; because constructs varied by condition, we saw pronounced differences in performance despite learning effects.

The task exercised essential aspects distinguishing Delta's config-based structure from a baseline approach: variable declaration, interaction configuration, labels, and derived computation. Participants received a starter implementation of an interactive kinetic energy formula ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$), where $K$ and $v$ were interactive---dragging $v$ updated the value of $K$---but $m$ was static. They were asked to make $m$ draggable (with values ranging from 0 to 10 in increments of 1, defaulting to 1), add a label displaying its current value and description, and ensure $K$ updated correctly based on both $m$ and $v$. A reference video demonstrated the target behavior. Participants were given 20 minutes per task, a limit determined as being likely to allow all users to finish based on pilot testing. Instead of a demonstration or practice session, participants were given written API tutorial materials they could reference freely throughout. These materials were the same length and quality across conditions.

\paragraph{Baseline} We compared Delta to a baseline implementation designed resemble the best case of what a React implementation would look like that achieves Delta-style outputs. To the extent possible, we separated unnecessary complexity and boilerplate into auxiliary files apart from the main file; the constructs remaining included 
minimal logic for defining React state variables for the formula, a LaTeX strings with variables wrapped in \texttt{\textbackslash cssId\{\}} references, and basic components with matching props for managing hover, drag, and display values. The resulting React file was 61 lines in the baseline compared to 40 lines for Delta.

\paragraph{Measures.} To assess speed and effort, we collected two kinds of data. We collected behavioral measures by analyzing screen recordings that to serve as proxies for speed and effort; these included \textit{completion time} and, to assess obstacles to using each library, and notes on irrelevant edits. To measure completion time, we had participants tell us when they believed they were finished and then we checked their work; participants were asked to continue if they had not actually completed the task. Those who exceeded the 20-minute limit were stopped and their time recorded as 20 minutes. We also collected self-report measures of ease and other experience factors. On a 7-point Likert scale (1 = baseline, 4 = about the same, 7 = Delta), they rated which library was (1) easier to complete the task with, (2) better suited for the task, and (3) a better match for the language they would use to describe the task. Participants also provided free-text explanations for each rating.

\paragraph{Analysis.} Because our data are paired and not assumed to be normally distributed, we use the two-tailed Wilcoxon signed-rank test for all behavioral comparisons. For subjective Likert ratings, we test whether responses differ significantly from the neutral midpoint (4) using two complementary approaches: a two-tailed sign test as our primary analysis, since it requires only direction of difference and no distributional assumptions, and a two-tailed one-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank test to verify results hold when considering magnitude of differences. We report Cohen's $d$ for paired samples as a measure of effect size where applicable.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Effect on time.}
Nearly all tasks (37 of 38, 97\%) were completed within the 20-minute time limit (participant P30 did not complete the baseline task). As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:completion_time}, Participants completed the task significantly faster with Delta ($\text{Mdn} = 5.4$ min, $\text{M} = 5.3$ min, $\text{SD} = 1.8$ min) than with the baseline ($\text{Mdn} = 9.4$ min, $\text{M} = 10.5$ min, $\text{SD} = 4.4$ min); Wilcoxon $W = 6.0$, $p < .001$, Cohen's $d = 1.54$. Of 19 participants, 16 were faster with Delta. The median speedup was $1.87\times$. This effect was robust to the ordering of the conditions ($p = .027$ for Delta first; $p = .004$ for baseline first), with a larger mean difference in task times when Delta was experienced second (more details in Section~\ref{sec:order-effect}).

\paragraph{Effect on effort}
Participants rated Delta significantly above the neutral midpoint on all three scales: ease ($\text{Mdn} = 7$, $\text{M} = 6.42$; 18 of 19 above neutral, sign test $p < .001$; Wilcoxon $W = 1.5$, $p < .001$), suitability ($\text{Mdn} = 6$, $\text{M} = 5.84$; 15 of 17 non-tied above neutral, sign test $p = .002$; Wilcoxon $W = 9.0$, $p = .001$), and language match ($\text{Mdn} = 7$, $\text{M} = 5.53$; 14 of 17 non-tied above neutral, sign test $p = .013$; Wilcoxon $W = 26.5$, $p = .014$). Eighteen of 19 participants rated Delta as easier (scores of 5--7); the one exception was P30, who valued the baseline's proximity to idiomatic React (``Library A felt easier to understand''). Six of 19 participants (32\%) attempted edits in irrelevant parts of the codebase with the baseline, compared to only 1 (5\%) with Delta. A further analysis of what in the baseline caused friction, and of natural and unnatural parts of Delta, appears in Section~\ref{sec:free-text-analysis}.
